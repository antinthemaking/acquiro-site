How a UK Premium Skincare Brand Generated £119K in Q4 From a Cold Meta Account

Acquiro helped a UK-based premium skincare brand expand from Google-only paid acquisition into Meta for the first time, generating £119,793 in Q4 2025 revenue on £34,976 of ad spend at a 3.42x return. The work was built around bringing the brand onto Meta cleanly, generating a new customer database for Black Friday and beyond, and giving the in-house team the campaign architecture to run it themselves once the foundations were in place.

The client is a UK-based premium skincare brand selling clean, cruelty-free formulations focused on skin regeneration, targeting a mature female demographic that takes ingredient quality and ethical sourcing seriously. The product range is sharp, the formulations are differentiated, and the brand had already built a real business on Google paid traffic across the continental European market.

When they came to us, the brief was specific. The team wanted to expand onto Meta for the first time at any meaningful scale, in time to build a fresh customer database heading into Q4 and Black Friday. Part of the wider business strategy was an acquisition play, and a strong DTC customer base was a critical asset in that conversation.

The in-house team was strong. They had a CMO, real revenue, and proven creative output. What they didn't have was Meta experience. They'd spent around £5,000 on the platform across the entire history of the account before we got involved, and the campaign structures, audience setups, and creative formats that work on Google don't translate one-to-one to Meta.

The engagement was structured around our done-for-you methodology. We came in, audited the full funnel, stripped out the existing setup, and rebuilt the campaign architecture and creative framework from scratch. The in-house team then ran the system day to day with our frameworks driving the work.

Challenge: Cracking Meta from a Google-native foundation

When we looked at the brand's setup heading into Q4, three things shaped the work.

  1. A Meta account with almost no spend history. £5,000 in lifetime spend on Meta meant the pixel had close to no data to work from. The algorithm was effectively starting cold, and we needed to generate enough signal quickly to make optimisation possible inside a Q4 window that had no room for slow ramp-up.
  2. Profitability had to come fast. The brief wasn't to spend big and learn slowly. The brief was to generate net new customers profitably, build a database for Black Friday, and prove out Meta as a viable second channel alongside Google. Every campaign had to be built to deliver against the first order economics from day one.
  3. The team needed frameworks, not just results. The in-house team was strong. The CMO was capable. What they needed was the Meta-specific campaign architecture, creative format guidance, and structural know-how to run the platform themselves once we'd built the foundations. The work wasn't just to spend the budget. It was to leave the team with a system they could continue scaling after the engagement.

The brand had everything in place to perform on Meta. The job was building the right architecture on top of it.

Process: Build the Meta-specific architecture, hand it to the team

The approach was a fast, focused done-for-you build, not a long retainer.

1. Foundation

The first piece was a full audit of the existing funnel. We pulled apart their product page experience, their storefront layout, their email and SMS flows, and the creative assets they'd been running on Google. The funnel got rebuilt where it needed rebuilding, product pages were optimised to lift conversion rate, and the storefront was restructured around the SKUs that were most likely to perform with cold Meta audiences.

In parallel, we got the tracking layer where it needed to be. Meta Pixel properly configured, custom audiences built across video viewers, profile engagers, website visitors, add-to-cart abandoners, and existing purchasers. Even with limited historical Meta data, we wanted the system to start tagging behaviour from day one of the new campaigns.

2. Engine

Once the foundations were in, we built out the Meta-specific campaign architecture across the formats the team hadn't worked with before.

Cold prospecting. Direct-response campaigns built around the brand's strongest creative assets, tested against cold audiences to find the angles that performed. The team's creative output was already high quality, which gave us strong inputs to work with. The job was structuring the prospecting layer to compound the data quickly.

DPA and catalogue campaigns. Dynamic product ads pulling from the full catalogue, retargeting site visitors with the products they'd actually looked at, and prospecting against cold audiences with the SKUs most likely to convert based on the data we had. This was a Meta-specific format the team hadn't used and one of the biggest unlocks in the build.

UGC and creative format guidance. We worked with the team on the type of content that performs natively on Meta versus what works on Google. UGC formats, founder-led content, ingredient-led stories, and the format-specific guidance that turns strong assets into strong ads.

3. Scaling into Q4

With the system live, the work shifted into running through Q4 and Black Friday. The architecture held, the campaigns performed profitably from the first cohort, and the customer database the brand was specifically trying to build was generated through the period.

Outcome: A profitable second channel, built fast

Across Q4 2025:

  • £119,793 ($148,500) in total revenue
  • £34,976 ($43,000) in total ad spend
  • 3.42× return on ad spend
  • £16.44 average cost per purchase
  • A net new customer database generated for Black Friday and beyond
  • A Meta campaign architecture the in-house team could continue running and scaling

The bigger shift is that the brand now operates with two profitable acquisition channels instead of one, with Meta sitting alongside Google as a proven second channel and the in-house team holding the frameworks to keep scaling spend without losing efficiency.

Lessons: What translates and what doesn't

A few things became clearer than ever working on this account.

Google playbooks don't transfer to Meta. A brand can be excellent at Google paid traffic and still be at a standing start on Meta. The platforms reward different things. The creative formats, the campaign structures, the audience logic, even the conversion mechanics behave differently. Brands that try to copy-paste a Google approach onto Meta usually leave the platform thinking it doesn't work for them, when the reality is the architecture just has to be built for Meta specifically.

A strong product team makes paid media easier. This account performed quickly in large part because the brand's in-house team was already capable of producing high-quality creative. When the creative inputs are strong, the paid media job becomes about architecture and optimisation rather than constantly chasing better assets. A strong product team is a multiplier on paid performance.

Done-for-you doesn't have to mean done forever. Some clients want a long-term agency partner running the day-to-day. Others want frameworks they can run themselves with strategic input. The right structure depends on the team. For this brand, a focused build with the in-house team picking up the day-to-day was the right model, and the engagement was structured around that explicitly.

Final thoughts

This brand wasn't broken when they came to us. They were a mature, capable DTC business with a profitable Google channel and a strong creative team. What they needed was the Meta-specific architecture to add a second profitable channel before Q4, and the in-house team has continued running it since.

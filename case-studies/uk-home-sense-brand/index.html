How a UK Homeware Brand Went From £400 to £26K in Monthly Revenue in 90 Days

Acquiro helped a UK-based homeware brand specialising in scented candles, premium trinkets, and curated lifestyle products go from £300 to £400 in monthly Meta ad spend with no real campaign structure to £26,644 in month-three revenue at a 4.09x return on ad spend. The 90-day done-for-you engagement built the acquisition system from scratch, ran the campaigns through to a profitable run rate, and handed the whole thing back to the team.

The client is a UK-based homeware brand selling scented candles, premium trinkets, and curated lifestyle pieces, operating as both a reseller of premium brands and a maker of their own product line. UK-only market, strong product taste, a real founder team, and the kind of early traction that suggested the brand could become something much bigger if the acquisition side was figured out.

When they came to us, they were doing roughly £10,000 in monthly revenue, mostly through a combination of organic, light Google traffic, and existing customer reorders. They'd touched Meta but only just. Their entire ad activity on the platform consisted of traffic campaigns running on a £300 to £400 monthly budget, with no campaign structure, no defined audiences, no creative testing framework, and no understanding of how Meta actually works as a performance channel.

The brief was straightforward. Prove out Meta as a channel, build the system, and hand it back to the team in 90 days. A done-for-you engagement structured as a foundation build rather than a long retainer, with the goal of leaving the team with everything they needed to scale it themselves once we were finished.

Challenge: A real brand with no acquisition foundation

When we looked at the setup, three things shaped the work.

  1. A Meta account with almost no useful data. £300 to £400 a month in untargeted traffic campaigns doesn't really qualify as paid media. The pixel had almost nothing to learn from, no proper conversion tracking, no custom audiences, and no structural setup to optimise against. The account was effectively starting from zero.
  2. Reseller margins required a higher ROAS bar. Part of the product range was made up of resold premium brands, which meant tighter margins than a pure own-brand business. To make the unit economics work, ROAS had to stay meaningfully above the standard DTC threshold, which ruled out the usual "scale first, optimise later" approach. Every campaign had to be efficient from the start.
  3. The hero product question wasn't answered. The brand had a wide product range across candles, trinkets, and curated pieces. None of it had been tested against cold traffic at any meaningful scale, so the first job was working out which collections and which hero SKUs would actually convert with audiences who had never heard of the brand before.

The brand had a real product, real taste, and a team that wanted to learn. What they didn't have was the acquisition system that turned any of that into something that could scale.

Process: Build the foundation, prove the channel, hand it over

The work was structured as a focused 90-day build across three tight phases.

1. Foundation

Week one was the audit and setup. We pulled apart the existing Meta account, restructured the pixel and tracking layer, built out custom audiences across video viewers, profile engagers, website visitors, add-to-cart abandoners, and existing purchasers, and got the foundational infrastructure into the shape it needed to be in to support a proper campaign architecture.

We also worked with the team on hero product selection. Which candles, which trinkets, which collections had the strongest visual hook, the cleanest price point, and the best margin profile to test against cold audiences first. Getting that call right shaped the rest of the engagement.

2. Engine

Once the foundations were in, the campaign architecture went live across three layers.

Cold prospecting. Direct-response campaigns running against cold audiences, testing creative angles, hero products, and offer mechanics to find what actually converted at scale. The job in the early weeks was generating signal fast, then doubling down on what was working.

Retargeting and re-engagement. Once the prospecting layer was producing data, we built out retargeting against site visitors, add-to-cart abandoners, and existing customers, plus re-engagement campaigns aimed at lifting repeat purchase rate and lifetime value.

Conversion funnel optimisation. In parallel with the campaign work, we worked on the conversion funnel itself. Product pages, add-to-cart flow, checkout experience. Even small improvements compound when paid traffic is hitting the site at scale, and the conversion side had to be ready to absorb the volume the campaigns were generating.

3. Handover

By the end of month three, the system was producing consistent monthly revenue at a profitable ROAS. The team had been walked through the architecture, the creative framework, and the optimisation logic, and the engagement transitioned out cleanly with the in-house team picking up the day-to-day from there.

Outcome: From £400 in spend to £26K in monthly revenue

Across the 90-day engagement:

  • £26,644 ($33,000) in month-three revenue from Meta
  • 4.09× return on ad spend
  • Monthly Meta ad spend scaled from £300 to £400 to a level that produced consistent profitable returns
  • Total monthly revenue lifted from roughly £10K to over £36K combined across channels
  • A complete Meta acquisition system handed over to the in-house team for ongoing scaling

The bigger shift was what the team understood by the end of it. They went from a brand that wasn't sure paid acquisition could work for them to a brand that had proven it, understood the architecture, and could see the runway to scale. For a business at this stage, that's a more meaningful outcome than the topline numbers suggest.

Lessons: What early-stage brands actually need

A few things became clearer than ever working on this account.

The zero-to-one is the hardest part. Most case studies you read are about scaling brands that are already proven, which is useful but skips the hardest stage. Getting an early-stage brand from no real paid activity to a working acquisition system is the part that actually changes the business, and it's the part most agencies don't want to touch. Going from £400 a month in unstructured spend to a proper campaign architecture is a bigger lift than going from £10K to £50K once the foundations are already in place.

Foundations beat scale every time. There's a temptation in early-stage paid media to throw money at the platform and hope the algorithm figures it out. It doesn't work. The work that matters in the first 30 days isn't spend volume, it's pixel setup, audience structure, hero product selection, creative direction, and conversion funnel readiness. Brands that skip the foundation work never get past the plateau they're trying to break through.

The product and the team matter as much as the numbers. We took this engagement on because the product was genuinely strong and the team wanted to learn. That combination is what makes a short, focused build actually pay off long after the engagement ends. The acquisition system we built is only valuable because the underlying business is one worth scaling, and that's something agencies tend to underweight when they screen clients by revenue alone.

Final thoughts

This brand isn't doing eight figures. They're doing real, profitable, growing revenue with a proper Meta system underneath them for the first time, which is a more meaningful turning point than most case studies capture. It's also the kind of work we end up doing most often: brands at the zero-to-one stage with real product, a real team, and the intent to build something serious. The architecture is built and the in-house team is running it from here.

How a European Activewear Brand Broke Past a £10K Plateau and Scaled to £559K in Revenue

Acquiro helped an EU-based women's activewear brand break through the spend ceiling they'd been stuck under for months. The client is a European women's activewear brand selling premium-feeling pieces at an accessible price point, targeting a specific demographic that's been underserved by the bigger names in the category. Strong product, strong positioning, sharp brand identity, marketing across the UK, Ireland, and the wider European market.

When they came to us, they had a clear problem. The business was working, but it wasn't scaling.

The team was running their ads in-house. The account was producing revenue, but every time they tried to push monthly spend past the £5,000 to £10,000 range, the unit economics fell apart. ROAS hovered around 1.7 to 1.8x, which sat just under the profit line once cost of goods sold was factored in. They were doing it themselves, learning as they went, and leaning heavily on Meta's native Advantage+ campaign structures to do the heavy lifting. The result was an account that was working in spite of how it was set up rather than because of it.

What they wanted was simple. Scale to £30 to £50K per month in spend, profitably, above a 2.5x ROAS after COGS. That's the kind of margin that lets a DTC brand reinvest into product, into market expansion, and into the next phase of growth.

That's where we came in.

Challenge: A spend ceiling the account couldn't break through

When we audited the account, three things explained the plateau.

  1. Average order value was sitting too low for the price point. The brand's price point was already competitive, which gave them a relatively strong cost per acquisition. But the AOV was sitting at a level where, even with cheap acquisition, margin was thin once COGS came out. Customers were buying single items rather than sets, bundles, or multi-piece purchases. The product range was built for collections, and the buying behaviour wasn't matching that.
  2. The campaign structure was leaving money on the table. The account was relying heavily on Meta's native Advantage+ shopping campaigns and broad audience setups without the manual structure underneath to control spend allocation properly. Some of that's fine once an account has enough data, but at the spend level the brand was sitting at, it wasn't enough. Budget wasn't being deployed where it could actually generate the best return, and the account had no proper retargeting or re-engagement architecture sitting alongside the prospecting layer.
  3. The product page and checkout experience wasn't built for the offer. Their entire commercial model was built around collections, sets, and seasonal drops. The site experience didn't reinforce that. Customers were landing on individual product pages with no clear path to add multiple items to the cart, no upsell mechanic, and no incentive structure pulling them toward higher-value baskets.

The brand had a good product, a competitive price, and a real audience. What they didn't have was the infrastructure to turn cheap acquisition into a scalable business.

Process: Fix the AOV, restructure the account, scale

The work was straightforward in principle, which is the right way for an e-commerce account to feel.

1. Foundation

The first piece was AOV. We worked on the product page and add-to-cart experience, layering in add-to-cart maximisers, bundle prompts, and clearer pathways to multi-item purchases. The product range was already built around collections, so the job was making the buying flow match the commercial intent. Custom funnels were built out on Replo for collection drops and seasonal sales to give specific campaigns dedicated landing experiences instead of pushing all traffic to the same product pages.

The second piece was tracking and audience structure. Meta Pixel properly configured, custom audiences across video viewers, profile engagers, website visitors, add-to-cart abandoners, and existing purchasers. Every meaningful behaviour got tagged so we could retarget and optimise off real signal.

2. Engine

With the foundations in place, we restructured the campaign architecture across three layers.

Prospecting. Cold acquisition campaigns built with a balance of manual audience setups and Meta's AI-driven optimisation. The key wasn't picking one approach over the other. It was finding the right balance for this specific account at this specific spend level. Pure Advantage+ wasn't enough on its own. Pure manual was leaving the AI's strengths on the table. The blend was what unlocked the scale.

Retargeting and re-engagement. The brand had a sizable customer base by the time we started working together, and none of it was being properly retargeted. We built out retargeting campaigns across abandoned carts, page viewers, and existing customers, plus re-engagement campaigns to bring lapsed buyers back for new collection drops.

Collection drops and seasonal sales. The brand's commercial calendar was built around drops and seasonal moments. We built dedicated campaign structures for these, with bespoke creative, bespoke funnels on Replo, and concentrated spend windows aligned to the launch calendar. These became some of the highest-ROAS periods in the account.

3. Scaling

With AOV up, campaign structure rebuilt, and retargeting properly running, scaling spend became a matter of holding efficiency steady while pushing the prospecting budget higher. The account moved from a £5 to £10K monthly plateau into the £30 to £50K range profitably, with ROAS holding above the 2.5x threshold the team needed after COGS to make scaling sustainable.

Outcome: From break-even to scalable growth

Across the engagement, the system has produced:

  • £559,755 ($695,000) in attributed revenue
  • £179,746 ($223,000) in total ad spend
  • 3.11× return on ad spend
  • Monthly profitable spend scaled approximately 5× from the previous ceiling
  • Average order value materially up across the account
  • A campaign structure built to scale internationally, with US expansion as the next phase

Lessons: What e-commerce actually rewards

A few things became clearer than ever working on this account.

Simple usually wins. There's a temptation in e-commerce to over-engineer the marketing stack. Layers of attribution tools, complex audience splits, twenty-five active campaigns at once. In most accounts, the things that move the needle are the simple ones. AOV, cost per acquisition, retention, and a clean campaign structure. Fix those, and most of the rest takes care of itself.

Cheap acquisition only matters if AOV holds its weight. A low cost per purchase looks great on the dashboard, but it's only meaningful if the average order value is high enough to absorb COGS and leave margin. The fastest way to scale a low-AOV account isn't to chase a lower CPA. It's to lift the AOV.

Advantage+ isn't a strategy on its own. Meta's AI-driven campaign types are genuinely powerful, but they're a tool, not a complete approach. The right setup for most growth-stage e-commerce accounts is a balance between manual control and AI-led optimisation, and that balance shifts depending on the account, the spend level, and the maturity of the data. Defaulting everything to Advantage+ usually leaves performance on the table.

Final thoughts

This brand had a strong product, a sharp price point, and a clear audience. What they didn't have was the campaign structure and the AOV mechanics to scale beyond their existing ceiling.

Three things changed it. AOV went up. The account got restructured. The scaling math finally worked.

Now they're heading into the US.

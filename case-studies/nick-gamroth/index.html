How Nick Gamroth Turned a 300K Following Into a Predictable Client Pipeline

Across 18 months, Acquiro helped Nick Gamroth, a Germany-based online performance coach for C-suite executives and high-level entrepreneurs, build a paid acquisition system that delivered 4,787 qualified leads, 400+ closed clients, and multiple six figures in client revenue. All from a starting position most coaches would envy on paper: an Instagram following of over 300,000 that wasn't converting at the level it should have been.

Nick Gamroth is an online performance and fitness coach based in Germany, with an Instagram following of over 300,000 people and an ICP made up of C-suite executives and high-level entrepreneurs. He's the kind of operator most coaches would look at and assume the business runs itself. Big following, clear niche, sharp positioning.

The reality was more nuanced.

By the time we started working together, Nick had built a genuinely impressive organic audience, but he felt the audience was fatigued. His content had been built around growing the following, not converting it, and he could feel the gap between the size of his platform and the size of his actual client pipeline. He wasn't in control of his lead flow. He was relying on the law of averages: a big audience eventually produces some buyers. But that's not a system. That's hope wearing the costume of a strategy.

What he wanted was simple. Consistency. Leads he could count on. A pipeline that wasn't dependent on whichever post happened to go viral that week.

That's the work we picked up.

Challenge: A 300K following with no system to convert it

When we looked at Nick's setup, three things stood out.

  1. The audience was big but fatigued. Years of content built for growth had produced exactly that, growth. But the same audience had been served the same kind of content for so long that the natural conversion rate from follower to buyer had stalled. The follow had stopped meaning what it used to.
  2. The content wasn't aligned with the ICP. Nick's content was getting attention from a wide range of people, but his actual ICP, C-suite executives and high-performing entrepreneurs, weren't necessarily the ones engaging most. The content was optimised for reach, not for resonance with the people who could afford and benefit from the work.
  3. The business had no paid layer. Every lead and every client came through organic. That meant Nick was at the mercy of the algorithm, the content cycle, and whatever the platform decided to do that month. There was no consistent, controllable channel bringing in qualified leads at predictable cost.

Nick didn't have an audience problem. He had a system problem. The audience was already there. What was missing was the architecture to turn it into a business that ran predictably.

Process: Realign the content, build the funnel, run paid media on top

The approach was to give Nick two things he didn't have before. Content aligned to his ICP, and a paid acquisition system that brought in qualified leads independently of what his organic was doing that week.

1. Foundation

The first piece was content realignment. We worked with Nick on shifting the content from vanity-driven to ICP-driven. Less optimising for views, more optimising for the C-suite executive who needs to feel that Nick understands their specific reality. That alone shifted the quality of inbound enquiries from the organic side.

In parallel, we built out the nurture asset, a long-form VSL hosted on ClickFunnels, designed to do the heavy lifting on warmer traffic and on prospects coming from his organic audience who needed more context before booking a call.

The final foundation piece was the tracking layer. Meta Pixel, custom audiences across video viewers, profile engagers, website visitors, and existing customer lists. Every meaningful behaviour got tagged so we could retarget and optimise off real signal.

2. Engine

Once the foundations were in, we layered paid media on top across three campaign types.

Direct lead generation on Meta. The main driver of volume. Native lead gen campaigns running directly on Meta, pre-qualifying prospects on the form itself and feeding qualified leads straight into Nick's pipeline. This was where the bulk of the 4,787 leads came from.

VSL nurture for warmer audiences. The ClickFunnels VSL sat behind a separate campaign track aimed at warmer traffic and organic followers. Prospects who needed more context before booking went through the VSL, came out at the other end pre-sold, and booked calls at a higher conversion rate than cold traffic would.

Top of funnel and retargeting. A consistent flow of cold traffic into the system to keep the audience pool refreshed, plus retargeting against everyone Nick's organic content had already touched. The combination kept lead cost stable as we scaled.

3. Stabilisation

Once spend hit a level Nick was comfortable with, the work shifted from scaling to stabilising. Nick didn't want a hockey stick. He wanted consistency. So the optimisation work focused on holding lead quality and cost steady at the spend level he wanted, while layering in seasonal campaigns and special offers periodically to drive bursts of demand.

That's a different game to most paid media work, and it's the right game for a coach who values predictability over speed.

Outcome: 18 months of predictable client flow

Across 18 months of paid media:

  • 4,787 qualified leads generated
  • 400+ clients closed off the back of those leads
  • Multiple six figures in client revenue generated
  • $122,000 ($112,000 EUR) in total ad spend across the engagement
  • A stable, predictable lead flow Nick controls, independent of organic performance

The bigger shift is that Nick now treats his organic content as the trust-building layer it should be, not the entire business. Social media is the gateway. The paid system is the engine. The two reinforce each other, and Nick is no longer dependent on either one alone to keep his calendar full.

Lessons: What a big following actually is, and isn't

A few things became clearer than ever working on Nick.

A big following isn't a business. This is the lesson every coach, creator, and personal brand needs to internalise. Most influencers don't make as much as the follower count would suggest because they've optimised for the wrong thing. They've built audiences of passive engagers instead of qualified buyers, and there's a massive difference between the two. Follower count is a vanity metric. Pipeline is a business metric.

Content has to be built for the people you actually want to attract. The biggest disconnect in personal brand marketing is the gap between who creators say they help and who their content is actually pulling in. If your ICP doesn't feel like you're speaking to them and for them, they won't build the trust that gets them to buy. Audit who your content is genuinely resonating with, not who you wish it was resonating with.

Once paid is dialled, scaling becomes the easy part. With the right funnel, the right creative, and a properly built campaign structure, scaling paid spend on Meta is much simpler than most operators realise. The hard work is the foundation. Once that's in place, going from $2K a month in spend to $20K a month is a question of when you want to, not whether you can.

Final thoughts

Nick didn't have a marketing problem. He had a pipeline predictability problem. A 300K following looks like an asset, and it is, but it isn't a system. The system is what we built around it.

Eighteen months later he has both. The audience and the engine.
